About PacketGuard

Network intrusion detection, trained and evaluated openly

01What This Is

PacketGuard sorts network traffic into five categories — Normal, DoS, Probe, R2L, and U2R — using a classifier trained on NSL-KDD, the standard academic benchmark for intrusion detection. Rather than reporting a single accuracy number from a notebook, it's a working tool: upload traffic, get a classified breakdown, see what the model looked at, and run a second, independent anomaly check on the same data.

The dashboard also shows every model that was tried along the way, not just the one that shipped, so the comparison in the Compare page is the actual development history, not a highlight reel.

02Key Finding

R2L and U2R recall stays low — in the 2–12% range — across every model tried, even after SMOTE oversampling and hyperparameter tuning. Feature-importance analysis shows the models lean on traffic-volume features (byte counts, connection counts) rather than the login/content features — num_failed_logins, root_shell, num_compromised — that R2L attacks actually depend on.

Why this matters
This points to a data-level ceiling rather than a tuning problem: NSL-KDD's KDDTest+ split intentionally includes attack subtypes that never appear in training, specifically to expose models that only memorize known patterns instead of generalizing. No amount of hyperparameter search fixes a gap in what the training data contains.
